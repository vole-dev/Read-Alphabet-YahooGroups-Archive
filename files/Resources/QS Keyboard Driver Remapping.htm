Comments:

  • Typing speed and ease of adapting from our current alphabet, rather than abstract logic, is the goal.
  • Most of the consonants in the present Roman alphabet are strongly (though not exclusively) associated with a single consonantal sound, except for c,q and x.  Therefore, the logical QS letters can easily be mapped to BDFGHJKLMNPRSTVWYZ.
  • Numerous QS letters were still available to fill the c, x, and q slots.  "Tall X," the more common sound, was a natural fit for the x key.  Only two remaining letters have variants, however, Thither and Whitewheat, which have the extended form and the half-form respectively.  Assigning them to the keyboard will prevent their alternate forms from having a more complicated mapping than other alternate forms.  Since C is the easier key to use, the extremely common letter, Thither is mapped to it, and Whitewheat is mapped to the Q.
  • All other characters will require a "flag" of at least two keystrokes to signify the character.  Shush, Church, Zhivago, Thoth, and Inkling are strongly associated with the digraphs SH, CH, ZH, TH, and NG in traditional orthography, so those will be used as the flags.

  • Any "impossible" QS letter combination may be used for flagging alternate forms. It's desirable to have a single letter to remember as the "key ingredient of the flag. J is the letter of choice for flagging an alternate QS consonantal form. J is the strongest finger of the right hand, and cannot occur after any consonant sound in English except R, L, and N. Since R and L have no variants, only the NJ presents a problem  An exception is NJ, as in change cEnJ. For NJ, substitute JN. X could've been chosen, but is a more difficult key to type, and H would recreate the already-used flags ZH, CH, TH, if coming second, and the HY sound occurs in some words if H comes first hVman. Also, H is involved in some of the Senior QS contractions,   An additional reason for choosing J is that it does not occur in any of Read's recommended "contractions," so all of them remain available for use, unlike most other letters. I considered doubling but rejected because in present and past tenses of some verbs, noun plurals, and also with some prefixes, showing the doubling QS letters is recommended.. andarrEtd, Add, plIzz, briNN, annOn.  (See QS manual 18-22.)

  • With computers, we are not limited to a single flag for a character.  As long as any flag has only one possible character, multiple flags can be assigned to a given character. Besides the XJ flag, the Deep X can be given an additional flag XZ, which approximates its sound.

  • The Namer Dot can be flagged by KJ, which would allow a remapped font to have the slash on the usual key, and would allow for rapid typing of a name beginning with an initial letter on the left-hand side of the keyboard, like Susan (KJSUSAN).. The alternative flag on the left-hand side, DF allows for rapid typing of a dot before a name beginning with an initial on the right-hand side, like Kyle (DFKYYL). DF and KJ fall under the strongest fingers on the home row, and with a little practice, should be substantially faster to use than the pinky on "/."Additionally, the need for using the Shift key is eliminated completely, except for some punctuation marks and symbols. Shaw intended for the new alphabet to have no "case," so I can imagine his typewriter had no use of Shift for alphabetic characters; neither will our keyboard driver.
  • Using a table of the frequency of Shavian characters, it is easy to see that short vowels are far more common than long ones, and are therefore a higher priority to put onto the single vowel keys.
  • Longer vowels and diphthongs are most easily flagged by their most typical spellings in Latin-alphabet English.  With the power of the computer, there is no need to limit a QS letter to a single flag.  Having multiple choices makes for easier adaptation.  The proper sound is more likely to be typeable by a quick series of keystrokes the typist already knows and is able to type quickly, often matching the current spelling of a word: rain, away, tree, sigh, etc. 
  • Junior QS fonts do not need a separate keyboard driver. The half-letters and alternate Thither, No, and Up would simply be ignored.
  • Theoretically, an "automatic" Senior QS keyboard driver could be created which would automatically choose the half-letters when certain other letters follow.  For instance, it would be possible to program TE as the flag for Half-Tut connecting to Ed and distinct from TO, which could flag the full Tut followed by Owe.  I do not believe this is desireable, since, besides needing a much, much greater number of flags (all possible combinations would need to be programmed) it would assume omnipresent use of half-and alternate letters as the norm.  I feel that the proper computer use of half-letters is for headings, cursive effects, word art, and special emphasis. If a driver were created which automatically inserted half letters whenever the preceded certain letters, an entirely separate driver would be needed for Junior QS, which would be an additional complication.  This suggested mapping has the advantage of allowing half-letters to easily be selected whenever desired, without making them mandatory, thus accommodating both Junior and Senior QS.
